In the Eastern Zhou period, a large portion of the inscriptions on coinage are the names of the mint towns. Huang Xiquan estimates that the number of locality inscriptions on square-foot spades, pointed-foot spades, and bridge-foot coins is approximately 160, 30, and 20, respectively.The locality inscriptions not only help identify the production cities but also provide a glimpse into the commercial activity of these cities. Therefore, identifying the location of the mint town is an essential step in studying the economic activities. 
Nevertheless, whether an inscription can be matched to a specific town—or even whether some inscriptions should be understood as locality names at all—remains a matter of ongoing debate. Although the historical geography research is not the main focus of this dissertation, it is essential to explain my principles in mapping the cities. 
Several clues can be used to decide whether or not the inscriptions can be understood as localities. (1) A large portion of the inscriptions precisely match the localities in transmitted texts and excavated texts. (2) One locality usually appeared on multiple media, such as bronze weapons, bronze vessels, pottery, and seals. (3) The format within one currency is usually stable. If the inscriptions on the same type of coins are mostly the names of the mint town, then the inscriptions that have not been deciphered might also indicate some unknown sites.  
Based on the three principles above, the inscriptions on the bridge-foot spades, pointed-foot spades, square-foot spades, sharp-angled spades, and Qi large knife coins certainly indicate the mint town. While most of the inscriptions on the pointed-tip knives, ming knives, and hollow-socket spades probably indicate the production institutions rather than localities. For example, on hollow-socket spades, the inscriptions often consist of numerals, stem-branch signs, and single-character inscriptions that cannot be matched to any known site. For example, Shaoqushinan, may indicate that the coins were issued south of the market in Shaoqu. In this case, the inscription refers to a specific institution or location within Shaoqu, rather than to the city itself. Another example is the pointed-tip knives. Although Huang Xiquan argued that some inscriptions represent specific groups or cities based on phonetic resemblance, none of the inscriptions on the pointed-tip knives match the city names found in other media. The inscriptions on the pointed-tip knives and ming knives share a lot of similarities, such as the use of numeral stem-branch signs, implying that these inscriptions might relate to production institutions rather than locations. 
For the locality inscriptions, although many match the sites in the texts, sometimes it is difficult to identify them with specific sites. First, some inscriptions contain phonetic substitution, in which one graph was used for another because of their similar pronunciation, such as Fuhu on the pointed-foot spades indicatingLvsi, a city in present-day Wutai County, or ge on the square-foot spades indicating dai, another city in Yu County.  Second, in some cases, multiple cities have the same name. For example, Anyang has been seen in Han, Zhao, Wei, and Qi. In this circumstance, it is hard to decide which city issued the coin with the Anyang inscription. Third, some place names change to other regions over time, while transmitted texts record only later locations, not earlier ones. For instance, Pingzhou County was first set in Yulin, and then moved to Lishi due to the invasion of the Xiongnu during the Eastern Han period. However, the transmitted texts only record Pingzhou in Shanxi, while neglecting its first location. Only in recent years, with the discovery of excavated texts, has it become possible to identify the original location of Pingzhou County.
The dissertation does not primarily focus on research in historical geography. In fact, previous research has already provided a foundation for our further studies. However, because there are often different explanations for the same city, it is still necessary for me to explain the principle for identifying the city's location. 
First, the excavated texts are the direct evidence for us to identify the city. For instance, pottery with the inscription Shan has been constantly found in several sites in present-day Shanzhou district, Sanmenxia, implying that this is the location of the Shan county in the Warring States period. In recent years, the discovery of pottery vessels bearing locality inscriptions in northern Shaanxi has provided new evidence for discussing the cities of the Loess Plateau.
Second, the circulation pattern can also give us clues to identify the mint town.Coins were usually first used locally and then circulated to other regions. As distance from the issuing city increased, the number of coins generally decreased. Zhu Hua used the percentage of Pingyang square-foot spades in hoards to argue that Pingyang should be located in southern Shanxi rather than Hebei, since Pingyang spades occur in significantly higher proportions in southern Shanxi than in other regions.
Third, the transmitted texts can offer clues about the location, but we need to clarify the historical changes. For instance, Cheng Enze identified Ganguanwith Fushi County, but he did not realize that the location of Fushi County in the Qing dynasty differed from that in the Qin and Han periods. As a result, he wrongly placed it near present-day Yan'an.
Here, I would try to present the location of each city in the transmitted texts. The location of the city is primarily based on geographical texts from before the Song Dynasty, such as Shuijingzhu, Kuodizhi, and Yuanhejunxianzhi. If the city can be matched to specific archaeological sites, I will place the city at that site. The city site is based on archaeological reports and the Atlas of Chinese Cultural Relics. However, for cities for which no clear clues are available, or for which the evidence is very vague, I will note the uncertainty in the text and provide only an approximate location. For some place name that can match multiple cities, I will list all the possibilities and evidence.

高都

King K’an enfeoffed his brother with Ho-nan thereby to prolong the position of the Duke of Zhou. “He (Duke Wei) then enfeoffed his youngest son with Kung 鞏, thereby to serve the king, and called him Duke Hui 惠 of East Chou(Sima 1994, 79).” The city located in present-day Kangdian Village, Kangdian Town, Gongyi City.
